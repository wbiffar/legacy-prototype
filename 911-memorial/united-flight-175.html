Sophia B. Addo

Sophia Addo arrived in New York from Ghana in her early twenties carrying a single suitcase and what her mother called “the kind of stubbornness that gets you somewhere.” She built a career in actuarial science at Marsh, sang every Sunday in the choir at Union Baptist in Harlem, and after work tutored neighborhood kids in algebra at the kitchen table she shared with her sister.

Sophia was 36. The scholarship her family established in her name has, in the years since, sent more than fifty young women from her hometown of Kumasi to university — a quiet, ongoing answer to a loss her family says still feels new.

Siew Nya Ang

Siew Nya Ang — “Cindy” to her colleagues, “Mama” to her two boys — came home from work each evening with the same ritual: shoes off at the door, tea on the stove, a moment by the kitchen window before anyone could ask her about her day. She had worked her way up at Marsh from a junior analyst to a senior risk consultant over fifteen years.

Her colleagues remember her, above all, for the careful way she could explain a complicated thing without making anyone feel small. She is survived by her husband, William, and their sons, Marcus and Dylan, who were 9 and 7 in 2001.

Mike Arczynski

When in London, where he and Mrs. Arczynski lived for nine years before returning to New York last year, the Alps became their playground. Back in the United States, he loved where the family settled, in Monmouth County, N.J., partly because from there he could take a bracing, high-speed ferry ride every day to work across New York Harbor to his job as a senior vice president at Aon, in 2 World Trade Center.

But the family is in a quandary now about what kind of tree would best represent him: Canadian Maple seemed perfect at first. “But I like the idea of an evergreen, too,” his wife said.

Jack Charles Aron

A graduate of the Wharton School with an irrepressible love of the New York Mets, Jack Aron worked the early shift at Cantor Fitzgerald so he could be home in time to coach his daughter’s softball team. He once drove four hours to Cooperstown on a Tuesday simply to walk the baseball Hall of Fame in the rain — alone, he said, because the place spoke better in the quiet.

Friends remember him as the dependable one — the friend who would loan you his car keys without a thought, then call later just to make sure you got home safely. He is survived by his wife of 22 years, Ellen, and their three children, Sarah, Daniel, and Charlotte.
